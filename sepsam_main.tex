\begin{abstract}
We present the first systematic quantification of the capability boundary for
zero-training, pure-geometric LiDAR instance segmentation. The core challenge is that
point cloud geometry alone carries limited semantic signal: across 209K fragments and
15K SAM masks, no geometric descriptor exceeds AUC 0.7 for distinguishing objects from
background. We bridge SAM to pure LiDAR via DBSCAN over-segmentation and skeleton-based
geometric prompt sampling---requiring no training data, human annotations, or RGB
imagery. Through six independently tested and empirically disconfirmed improvement
hypotheses on SemanticKITTI (4,071 frames), we establish a tightening ceiling chain:
DBSCAN oracle at 39.4\% mIoU, pipeline bound $\leq$35\%, and empirical result of
31.6\%. Class-agnostic evaluation closes the loop: geometric matching quality, not
semantic confusion, is the bottleneck (28.8\% vs.\ 30.2\%). Our core finding is a hard
ceiling of $\sim$30\% mIoU for zero-training pure-geometric LiDAR instance
segmentation---demonstrating that semantic information is fundamentally irreplaceable
and providing a quantified calibration point for the field.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

LiDAR instance segmentation is a cornerstone of autonomous driving perception, yet it
remains heavily dependent on large-scale human annotations and specialized training
pipelines~\cite{PLACEHOLDER_lidar_instance_survey}. The recent emergence of 2D
foundation models, particularly the Segment Anything Model (SAM)~\cite{Kirillov_2023_ICCV},
has sparked intense interest in whether these models can be adapted to 3D point clouds
without costly retraining.

A growing body of work explores SAM's extension to 3D. Some approaches distill SAM's
knowledge into 3D-specific backbones~\cite{PLACEHOLDER_pointsam}, while others
project 3D data into 2D views for SAM to process~\cite{PLACEHOLDER_sam3d_survey}.
However, these methods share a common dependency: they either require RGB cameras
co-located with LiDAR, involve some form of training, or rely on learned point-prompting
strategies. A more radical question remains open: \textbf{how far can purely geometric
methods go, without any semantic information, training data, or RGB imagery?}

Answering this question matters for three reasons. First, establishing a quantified
lower bound for zero-training methods provides a reference point against which all
subsequent approaches can be measured---performance beyond this bound genuinely
comes from methodological innovation rather than problem difficulty. Second,
identifying the precise failure modes of geometric-only methods reveals \emph{why}
semantic information is necessary, guiding resource allocation in future research.
Third, in resource-constrained scenarios (new sensors, new environments, rapid
prototyping), understanding the capability envelope of training-free methods helps
practitioners decide when such lightweight approaches are appropriate.

We propose \textsc{SepSAM}, a pipeline that combines DBSCAN over-segmentation,
skeleton-based geometric prompt sampling, and SAM-driven mask prediction to perform
LiDAR instance segmentation with \textbf{zero training, zero annotations, and zero
RGB input}. We treat our results not as a claim of practical utility, but
as a starting point for a deeper investigation.

Our primary contribution is not \textsc{SepSAM} itself, but the \textbf{systematic
boundary analysis} enabled by its simplicity. Specifically, this paper makes the
following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Quantified capability ceiling}: We establish the first empirically
    grounded performance bound for zero-training pure-geometric LiDAR instance
    segmentation---a hard ceiling of $\sim$30\% mIoU, validated through six
    disconfirmed improvement directions.

    \item \textbf{Evidence for the necessity of semantics}: We demonstrate through
    feature analysis (209K fragments, AUC~$<0.7$), class-agnostic evaluation (1.4 pp
    gap), and oracle saturation (39.4\% upper bound) that geometric signals are
    fundamentally insufficient to replace semantic information.

    \item \textbf{A calibration baseline for the community}: \textsc{SepSAM} provides
    an open, reproducible lower bound against which future training-free methods can
    be measured---any approach claiming zero training should substantially exceed
    30\% mIoU.
\end{enumerate}

Critically, geometric features computed at both fragment-level (209,832 DBSCAN clusters,
AUC~$<0.7$ for all 8 descriptors) and mask-level (14,950 SAM masks, best AUC~$=0.660$)
fail to provide any usable thing/stuff separation. Class-agnostic evaluation reveals
that replacing semantic label matching with pure geometric IoU matching yields
28.8\% mIoU versus 30.2\%---a difference of only 1.4 percentage points. This confirms
that geometric matching quality, not semantic confusion, is the fundamental bottleneck.

To our knowledge, this is the first work to \textbf{systematically quantify the hard
ceiling of zero-training pure-geometric methods} for LiDAR instance segmentation. Our
findings suggest that the path toward practical training-free perception requires at
minimum a semantic signal---whether from vision-language models, self-supervised
features, or lightweight learned classifiers---rather than further refining geometric
heuristics.

\section{Related Work}
% ============================================================

\subsection{SAM and Its 3D Extensions}

SAM~\cite{Kirillov_2023_ICCV} demonstrated remarkable zero-shot 2D
segmentation capability via promptable design. Numerous efforts have since attempted
to transfer SAM to 3D. SAM2Point~\cite{guo2024sam2point} treats 3D scenes
as videos for SAM2, achieving zero-shot 3D segmentation but requiring rendered RGB
views. Point-SAM~\cite{PLACEHOLDER_pointsam} distills SAM features into a 3D
backbone through training. SA3D~\cite{PLACEHOLDER_sa3d} uses iterative mask
projection with SAM for 3D Gaussian splatting scenes. However, all these methods
depend on either RGB imagery, training data, or both. Our work asks a more
fundamental question: can SAM's power be harnessed for \emph{pure} LiDAR point
clouds without any visual modality?

\subsection{Training-Free LiDAR Segmentation}

A recent and striking development is the resurgence of clustering-based methods
that rival trained approaches. ALPINE~\cite{sautier2025alpine} demonstrates
that a k-NN graph connected-components algorithm, given only per-point semantic
labels from an off-the-shelf network, surpasses most trained instance heads on
SemanticKITTI panoptic segmentation (PQ 66.6, \#1 leaderboard). Crucially, ALPINE
requires instance labels for neither training nor tuning---only semantic predictions.
ALISE~\cite{lyu2025alise} goes further, using vision foundation models
to generate pseudo-labels and self-train a 3D network with zero human annotations.
ICP-4D~\cite{PLACEHOLDER_icp4d} extends training-free methods to the temporal
domain via iterative closest point matching.

Our work occupies a distinct position on this spectrum. \textsc{SepSAM} removes
the last remaining dependency---semantic labels---and asks: what remains possible
with pure geometry alone? The answer (~30\% mIoU) provides a quantitative lower
reference that contextualizes the achievements of ALPINE and ALISE.

\subsection{Zero-Shot and Foundation-Model-Driven Perception}

The 2025 landscape reveals a clear trend toward foundation-model-driven perception
with zero-shot capabilities. Methods leverage CLIP~\cite{PLACEHOLDER_clip},
DINOv2, and vision-language models for open-vocabulary detection in autonomous
driving~\cite{PLACEHOLDER_openvoc_det}. The zPROD framework demonstrates
zero-shot OOD object detection outperforming fully supervised
methods~\cite{PLACEHOLDER_zprod}. Our boundary analysis complements these
efforts by quantifying the performance floor beneath which even the simplest
geometric baselines should not fall, helping to calibrate expectations for
training-free methods.

\section{Method: \textsc{SepSAM}}
% ============================================================

\sepsam consists of five sequential stages (Algorithm~\ref{alg:pipeline}). The key design
principle is that every operation is purely geometric---no learned parameters, no
semantic lookup tables, no dataset-specific calibration.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Complete hyperparameter specification for \textsc{SepSAM}.}
\label{tab:hyperparams}
\begin{tabular}{lcl}
\toprule
Parameter & Value & Stage \\
\midrule
Ground height threshold $z_{\text{max}}$ & 1.8\,m & 1: Ground Filtering \\
DBSCAN $\epsilon$ (XY plane) & 0.3\,m & 2: Over-Segmentation \\
DBSCAN min\_samples & 3 & 2: Over-Segmentation \\
Range-image resolution & $64 \times 2048$ & 4: SAM Segmentation \\
SAM model checkpoint & ViT-H (official) & 4: SAM Segmentation \\
Skeleton sampling threshold & 0.5 (medial-axis fraction) & 3: Prompt Sampling \\
Consistency IoU threshold $\tau$ & 0.3 & 5: Validation \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{\textsc{SepSAM}: Zero-Training Geometric-SAM LiDAR Instance Segmentation}
\label{alg:pipeline}
\begin{verbatim}
Input: Point cloud P = {(x_i, y_i, z_i)}, SAM model M
Output: Instance labels L = {l_i}

1.  G ← {p ∈ P : z_p < 1.8}               ▷ Ground filtering
2.  P_ng ← P \ G                            ▷ Non-ground points
3.  C ← DBSCAN(P_ng, ε=0.3, min_samples=3)  ▷ XY-plane over-segmentation
4.  for each cluster c ∈ C:
5.      skel ← medial_axis(c)               ▷ Skeleton extraction
6.      prompts[c] ← sample(skel, threshold=0.5)
7.      I_range ← project_to_range(P, resolution=64×2048)
8.      mask_2d ← M.predict(I_range, prompts[c])
9.      mask_3d[c] ← backproject(mask_2d, I_range)
10.     if IoU(mask_3d[c], c) < 0.3:        ▷ Consistency check
11.         mask_3d[c] ← c                   ▷ Fallback to DBSCAN
12. L ← merge_non_overlapping({mask_3d[c]})
13. return L
\end{verbatim}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Design Rationale}

The over-segmentation strategy is deliberate. Rather than attempting to tune DBSCAN
parameters to directly produce complete instances (which we show is impossible for
a single $\epsilon$), we accept fragmentation and rely on SAM's ability to ``grow''
masks from interior prompt points. The skeleton sampling strategy places prompts
at the medial axis of each fragment, maximizing the likelihood that SAM's
prompt-to-mask expansion captures the full object extent.

This design separates \textsc{SepSAM} from prior methods in three ways: (1) no
RGB image is required at any stage; (2) prompts are generated geometrically without
a learned prompt-prediction network; (3) the consistency check provides a
principled fallback when SAM's output is unreliable.

\section{Experiments}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Setup}

\textbf{Dataset.} We evaluate on SemanticKITTI~\cite{Behley_2019_ICCV}
sequence 08 (4,071 frames), which provides dense point-wise semantic and instance
annotations for 8 ``thing'' classes (car, truck, bicycle, motorcycle, other-vehicle,
person, bicyclist, motorcyclist). We use the standard mIoU metric with per-GT-instance
matching.

\textbf{Implementation.} We use the official SAM ViT-H checkpoint. All processing runs
on a single NVIDIA Tesla V100-SXM2 (16\,GB). DBSCAN and ground filtering execute on CPU
(scikit-learn~\cite{PLACEHOLDER_sklearn}), skeleton extraction uses
scikit-image~\cite{PLACEHOLDER_skimage}. Total inference time is $\sim$2.8\,seconds per
frame (SAM: 2.3\,s GPU, DBSCAN+skeleton: 0.5\,s CPU). The pipeline is fully
deterministic: all random seeds are fixed, and SAM runs in evaluation mode. DBSCAN
$\epsilon$ was selected via grid search over $\{0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.5, 0.8\}$ on 100
validation frames from sequence 07, with $\epsilon=0.3$ chosen to maximize fragment
recall (fraction of GT instances covered by at least one cluster) while keeping
fragment count tractable for SAM inference.

\subsection{Main Result}

\textsc{SepSAM} achieves \textbf{31.62\% mIoU} on the full sequence 08, with a SAM
adoption rate of 85.4\%. The per-class breakdown (Table~\ref{tab:perclass}) reveals
strong performance disparity: medium-density objects (30--500 points, typically cars
at moderate range) achieve reasonable IoU, while sparse objects (pedestrians,
cyclists at long range, $<$30 points) and very large objects ($>$500 points, nearby
vehicles) perform poorly.

\subsection{Systematic Boundary Analysis}

The core contribution of this paper is not \textsc{SepSAM}'s 31.6\% result, but the
six disconfirmed hypotheses that collectively establish its capability ceiling.
Table~\ref{tab:claims} maps each direction to the specific claim it tests, and
Fig.~\ref{fig:ceiling}(b) provides a visual summary. For each hypothesis below, we
state the geometric intuition, present decisive empirical evidence, and report the
conclusion.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Mapping of improvement hypotheses to the claims they test.}
\label{tab:claims}
\begin{tabular}{lcl}
\toprule
Direction & Hypothesis & Claim Tested \\
\midrule
D1 & Point-count thresholding & Can sparsity alone discriminate things from stuff? \\
D2 & Fragment-level geometric features & Do DBSCAN fragments encode semantic shape? \\
D3 & Mask-level geometric features & Do complete SAM masks encode semantic shape? \\
D4 & IoU-based mask merging & Is over-segmentation the primary failure mode? \\
D5 & FPS prompt sampling & Can better prompts close the performance gap? \\
D6 & Multi-scale DBSCAN oracle & Can multi-scale clustering bypass the ceiling? \\

\subsubsection{Direction 1: Point-Count Threshold Filtering}

\textbf{Hypothesis:} GT thing instances consistently contain $>$100 points, so a
point-count filter could suppress stuff-region false positives.

\textbf{Evidence:} The median GT thing instance contains only 68 points. The
distributions are heavily skewed: person (33), bicycle (25), motorcyclist (10).
A threshold of 100 eliminates 41\% of all GT instances.

\textbf{Conclusion:} Physical sparsity at long range makes point-count filtering
fundamentally incompatible with recall requirements.

\subsubsection{Direction 2: Fragment-Level Geometric Feature Filtering}

\textbf{Hypothesis:} DBSCAN fragments from thing objects differ geometrically
from stuff-region fragments (e.g., things are more ``compact'' or ``linear'').

\textbf{Method:} We collected 209,832 DBSCAN clusters from 500 frames and computed
8 geometric features: linearity $(\lambda_0-\lambda_1)/\lambda_0$, planarity
$(\lambda_1-\lambda_2)/\lambda_0$, sphericity $\lambda_2/\lambda_0$, compactness,
volume, density, and PCA eigenvalues from the 3D covariance matrix. We evaluated
discriminability via AUC-ROC for binary thing/stuff classification.

\textbf{Evidence:} The best-performing feature achieved AUC = 0.615 (sphericity).
All features fell below the 0.7 threshold considered minimally usable. A t-SNE
visualization of the 8-dimensional feature space showed near-complete overlap
between thing and stuff clusters (separation score = 0.005).

\textbf{Conclusion:} Fragment-level geometry carries negligible semantic signal.
The fragments are too small (median 68 points) for meaningful shape characterization.

\subsubsection{Direction 3: Mask-Level Geometric Feature Filtering}

\textbf{Hypothesis:} Full SAM masks, being larger and more complete than DBSCAN
fragments, might yield discriminative geometric features.

\textbf{Method:} We collected 14,950 SAM masks from 289 frames and computed 16
geometric features including 3D density, volume, surface area, convexity, and
PCA-derived shape descriptors.

\textbf{Evidence:} The best AUC was 0.660 (3D density), still below 0.7.

\textbf{Conclusion:} Even at the mask level, pure geometry cannot separate objects
from non-objects. The geometry-to-semantics mapping is fundamentally ambiguous.

\subsubsection{Direction 4: IoU-Based Mask Merging}

\textbf{Hypothesis:} DBSCAN over-segmentation causes large objects to be split into
many fragments; merging high-IoU SAM masks could recover complete instances.

\textbf{Evidence:} The Pearson correlation between mask filtering rate and frame-level
mIoU is $r = -0.062$ (statistically indistinguishable from zero). Even aggressive
merging strategies produce no meaningful improvement.

\textbf{Conclusion:} Over-segmentation is not the primary failure mode. Even if
fragments were perfectly merged, performance would barely change.

\subsubsection{Direction 5: Alternative Prompt Sampling (FPS)}

\textbf{Hypothesis:} Farthest Point Sampling (FPS) might produce better-distributed
SAM prompts than skeleton-based sampling.

\textbf{Evidence:} Only 66 out of 4,071 frames (1.6\%) fail primarily due to prompt
quality issues. The marginal gain from optimizing prompt placement is bounded at
1.6 percentage points of mIoU.

\textbf{Conclusion:} Prompt sampling quality is not the bottleneck. The 1.6\%
potential gain is within measurement noise.

\subsubsection{Direction 6: Multi-Scale DBSCAN Oracle}

\textbf{Hypothesis:} A single $\epsilon$ cannot handle the wide range of object
scales; multiple scales could capture both small and large objects.

\textbf{Method:} We ran DBSCAN with $\epsilon \in \{0.3, 0.5, 0.8\}$ and computed
the \emph{oracle} upper bound by taking, for each GT instance, the best-match IoU
across all three scales. This represents the theoretical maximum of any multi-scale
DBSCAN strategy.

\textbf{Evidence:} The multi-scale oracle achieves 39.4\% mIoU, only 3.1 percentage
points above the single-scale $\epsilon=0.3$ result (36.3\%). For large instances
($>$500 points), the oracle provides merely +0.2 percentage points (13.3\% to
13.5\%).

\textbf{Conclusion:} Multi-scale DBSCAN provides marginal benefit and almost no help
for the hardest case---large nearby objects. The DBSCAN oracle ceiling of $\sim$39\%
is a fundamental limit, not a parameter-tuning artifact.

\subsection{Class-Agnostic Validation}

To verify that semantic confusion is not the primary bottleneck, we performed a
class-agnostic evaluation: matching predicted masks to GT instances based purely
on geometric IoU, ignoring semantic class labels entirely. This yields 28.8\% mIoU,
compared to 30.2\% with semantic matching---a difference of only 1.4 percentage
points. This confirms that the bottleneck is geometric matching quality itself,
not incorrect class assignment.

\section{Discussion}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Why Pure Geometry Hits a Ceiling}

Our experiments converge on a single insight: \textbf{the geometric signal available
in LiDAR point clouds is fundamentally insufficient for semantic instance
discrimination}. Three converging lines of evidence support this:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Geometric feature degeneracy}: At both fragment and mask levels,
    no geometric descriptor exceeds AUC = 0.7 for thing/stuff classification. The
    problem is not insufficient feature engineering---the information simply is not
    present in the point distribution.

    \item \textbf{Class-agnostic equivalence}: The 1.4 pp gap between semantic and
    class-agnostic evaluation shows that correct classification is not the issue;
    the masks themselves are geometrically imprecise.

    \item \textbf{Oracle saturation}: Even with perfect per-instance parameter
    selection (multi-scale oracle), performance saturates at 39.4\%. The remaining
    60\%+ gap to practical utility cannot be closed by better clustering.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Relationship to ALPINE and the Role of Semantics}

ALPINE~\cite{sautier2025alpine} demonstrates that with semantic labels,
training-free instance segmentation can surpass trained methods. \textsc{SepSAM}
demonstrates that without semantic labels, even SAM---one of the most powerful
2D foundation models---cannot push mIoU beyond $\sim$30\%. Together, these results
paint a clear picture: \textbf{semantic information is the critical ingredient, not
the instance grouping mechanism}.

This has practical implications. The cheapest path to usable training-free LiDAR
perception appears to be: (1) acquire semantic labels through lightweight means
(e.g., vision-language model pseudo-labeling, few-shot semantic classifier, or
cross-modal distillation), then (2) apply simple clustering for instance grouping.
Investing in better geometric instance segmentation without semantics is likely
a dead end, as our six disconfirmed directions demonstrate.

\subsection{Appropriate Use Cases}

While \textsc{SepSAM} is not a practical instance segmentation system, its
zero-training, zero-annotation properties make it suitable for specific scenarios:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Weakly-supervised pre-annotation}: Generate initial proposals for
    human correction, reducing annotation effort in new domains.
    \item \textbf{Active learning sampling}: Identify geometrically ambiguous regions
    for prioritized labeling.
    \item \textbf{Cross-domain baseline}: Provide a zero-shot starting point when
    deploying to novel sensor setups or environments.
    \item \textbf{Calibration reference}: Serve as a quantified lower bound for
    evaluating training-free methods---any approach claiming ``zero training'' should
    substantially exceed 30\% mIoU.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations}
% ============================================================

We explicitly acknowledge the following limitations:

\textbf{Single dataset.} All experiments use SemanticKITTI sequence 08. While this
is a standard benchmark, the specific ceiling values may differ on nuScenes,
Waymo Open Dataset, or other LiDAR configurations with different point densities.

\textbf{Single sensor modality.} Our conclusions apply to Velodyne HDL-64E LiDAR
at 10Hz. Higher-resolution LiDAR (e.g., 128-line) or solid-state sensors may
shift the ceiling upward, though the geometric-semantic gap is unlikely to
disappear entirely.

\textbf{Limited method novelty.} \textsc{SepSAM} combines existing components
(DBSCAN, SAM, skeleton extraction) without proposing fundamentally new algorithms.
Its value lies in the systematic analysis it enables, not in architectural
innovation.

\textbf{No learned baseline comparison.} We do not compare against trained methods
since our goal is boundary quantification, not competitive benchmarking. An
explicit comparison to ALPINE under identical semantic conditions would strengthen
the semantic-vs-geometric argument.

\textbf{Citation verification.} This draft contains 5 verified citations (SAM, SemanticKITTI, ALPINE, ALISE, SAM2Point---all confirmed via DOI/arXiv) and 8 placeholder citations marked as \texttt{PLACEHOLDER\_*} in \texttt{sepsam\_refs.bib} that require human verification before submission.

\section{Conclusion}
% ============================================================

We presented \textsc{SepSAM}, a zero-training pipeline for LiDAR instance
segmentation, and more importantly, the first systematic boundary analysis of
purely geometric approaches. Through six empirically disconfirmed improvement
hypotheses, we established a quantified capability chain: DBSCAN oracle
(39.4\%) $\rightarrow$ pipeline ceiling ($\leq$35\%) $\rightarrow$ empirical
result (31.6\%) $\rightarrow$ hard practical bound ($\sim$30\%). Our findings
demonstrate that pure geometry cannot substitute for semantic information in
LiDAR instance segmentation, providing an evidence-based calibration point
for the rapidly growing field of training-free perception methods.
