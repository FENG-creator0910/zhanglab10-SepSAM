\begin{abstract}
We present the first systematic quantification of the capability boundary for
zero-training, pure-geometric LiDAR instance segmentation. The core challenge is that
point cloud geometry alone carries limited semantic signal: across 209K fragments and
15K SAM masks, no geometric descriptor exceeds AUC 0.7 for distinguishing objects from
background. We bridge SAM to pure LiDAR via DBSCAN over-segmentation and skeleton-based
geometric prompt sampling---requiring no training data, human annotations, or RGB
imagery. Through six independently tested and empirically disconfirmed improvement
hypotheses on SemanticKITTI (4,071 frames), we establish a tightening ceiling chain:
DBSCAN oracle at 39.4\% mIoU, pipeline bound $\leq$35\%, and empirical result of
31.6\%. Class-agnostic evaluation closes the loop: removing semantic class labels
changes mIoU by only 0.3 percentage points (29.9\% vs.\ 30.2\%), confirming
that geometric matching quality, not semantic confusion, is the bottleneck. Our core finding is a hard
ceiling of $\sim$30\% mIoU for zero-training pure-geometric LiDAR instance
segmentation---demonstrating that semantic information is fundamentally irreplaceable
and providing a quantified calibration point for the field.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

LiDAR instance segmentation is a cornerstone of autonomous driving perception, yet it
remains heavily dependent on large-scale human annotations and specialized training
pipelines~\cite{dusi2025lidar}. The recent emergence of 2D
foundation models, particularly the Segment Anything Model (SAM)~\cite{Kirillov_2023_ICCV},
has sparked intense interest in whether these models can be adapted to 3D point clouds
without costly retraining.

A growing body of work explores SAM's extension to 3D. Some approaches distill SAM's
knowledge into 3D-specific backbones~\cite{zhou2024pointsam}, while others
project 3D data into 2D views for SAM to process~\cite{guo2024sam2point,cen2023segment}.
However, these methods share a common dependency: they either require RGB cameras
co-located with LiDAR, involve some form of training, or rely on learned point-prompting
strategies. A more radical question remains open: \textbf{how far can purely geometric
methods go, without any semantic information, training data, or RGB imagery?}

Answering this question matters for three reasons. First, establishing a quantified
lower bound for zero-training methods provides a reference point against which all
subsequent approaches can be measured---performance beyond this bound genuinely
comes from methodological innovation rather than problem difficulty. Second,
identifying the precise failure modes of geometric-only methods reveals \emph{why}
semantic information is necessary, guiding resource allocation in future research.
Third, in resource-constrained scenarios (new sensors, new environments, rapid
prototyping), understanding the capability envelope of training-free methods helps
practitioners decide when such lightweight approaches are appropriate.

We propose \textsc{SepSAM}, a pipeline that combines DBSCAN over-segmentation,
skeleton-based geometric prompt sampling, and SAM-driven mask prediction to perform
LiDAR instance segmentation with \textbf{zero training, zero annotations, and zero
RGB input}. We treat our results not as a claim of practical utility, but
as a starting point for a deeper investigation.

Our primary contribution is not \textsc{SepSAM} itself, but the \textbf{systematic
boundary analysis} enabled by its simplicity. Specifically, this paper makes the
following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Quantified capability ceiling}: We establish the first empirically
    grounded performance bound for zero-training pure-geometric LiDAR instance
    segmentation---a hard ceiling of $\sim$30\% mIoU, validated through six
    disconfirmed improvement directions.

    \item \textbf{Evidence for the necessity of semantics}: We demonstrate through
    feature analysis (209K fragments, AUC~$<0.7$), class-agnostic evaluation (0.3 pp
    gap), and oracle saturation (39.4\% upper bound) that geometric signals are
    fundamentally insufficient to replace semantic information.

    \item \textbf{A calibration baseline for the community}: \textsc{SepSAM} provides
    an open, reproducible lower bound against which future training-free methods can
    be measured---any approach claiming zero training should substantially exceed
    30\% mIoU.
\end{enumerate}

Critically, geometric features computed at both fragment-level (209,832 DBSCAN clusters,
AUC~$<0.7$ for all 8 descriptors) and mask-level (14,950 SAM masks, best AUC~$=0.660$)
fail to provide any usable thing/stuff separation. Class-agnostic evaluation on 100 uniformly sampled frames reveals
that replacing semantic label matching with pure geometric IoU matching yields
30.2\% mIoU versus 29.9\%---a negligible difference of 0.3 percentage points. This confirms
that geometric matching quality, not semantic confusion, is the fundamental bottleneck.

To our knowledge, this is the first work to \textbf{systematically quantify the hard
ceiling of zero-training pure-geometric methods} for LiDAR instance segmentation. Our
findings suggest that the path toward practical training-free perception requires at
minimum a semantic signal---whether from vision-language models, self-supervised
features, or lightweight learned classifiers---rather than further refining geometric
heuristics.

\section{Related Work}
% ============================================================

\subsection{SAM and Its 3D Extensions}

SAM~\cite{Kirillov_2023_ICCV} demonstrated remarkable zero-shot 2D
segmentation capability via promptable design, sparking a wave of 3D adaptation
efforts. These adaptations fall into two broad strategies. \textbf{Projection-based}
methods render 3D data into 2D views for SAM to process: SAM2Point~\cite{guo2024sam2point}
treats 3D scenes as videos, and SA3D~\cite{cen2023segment} uses iterative mask
projection for radiance field scenes---both requiring rendered RGB imagery.
\textbf{Distillation-based} methods, such as Point-SAM~\cite{zhou2024pointsam},
train a 3D-native backbone to mimic SAM's 2D outputs, trading zero-shot generality
for 3D efficiency. Across both strategies, a common dependency persists: either RGB
cameras co-located with the 3D sensor, training data for distillation, or both. Our
work investigates the extreme end of this design space---harnessing SAM for
\emph{pure} LiDAR point clouds without any visual modality, training data, or learned
prompting strategy.

\subsection{Training-Free LiDAR Segmentation}

A recent and striking development is the resurgence of clustering-based methods
that rival trained approaches, forming a spectrum of dependency reduction.
At one end, ALPINE~\cite{sautier2025alpine} demonstrates that a k-NN graph
connected-components algorithm, given only per-point semantic labels from an
off-the-shelf network, achieves state-of-the-art panoptic segmentation on
SemanticKITTI (PQ 66.6, \#1 leaderboard) without instance-level training.
Moving further, ALISE~\cite{lyu2025alise} eliminates \emph{human} annotations
entirely by using vision foundation models to generate pseudo-labels for
self-training a 3D network. Orthogonally, ICP-4D~\cite{oh2025trainingfree}
extends the training-free paradigm to the temporal domain via iterative closest
point matching across 4D LiDAR sequences.

Our work occupies the extreme endpoint of this dependency-reduction spectrum.
\textsc{SepSAM} removes the last remaining dependency---semantic labels---and asks:
what remains possible with pure geometry alone? The answer ($\sim$30\% mIoU) provides
a quantitative lower reference that contextualizes the achievements of ALPINE and
ALISE: semantic information, not the instance grouping mechanism, accounts for the
performance gap between 30\% and 66\% PQ.

\subsection{Zero-Shot and Foundation-Model-Driven Perception}

Beyond clustering-based approaches, the broader perception landscape is shifting
toward foundation-model-driven methods with zero-shot generalization. Vision-language
models such as CLIP~\cite{radford2021learning} enable open-vocabulary 3D scene
understanding in autonomous driving without per-category training~\cite{cheng2024open3dworld}.
At the extreme, zPROD~\cite{sinhamahapatra2025zprod} demonstrates that frozen large
vision-language models can perform zero-shot out-of-distribution object detection,
outperforming fully supervised baselines on standard anomaly benchmarks. These
developments signal that foundation models are fundamentally reshaping the
capability envelope of perception systems.

Our boundary analysis engages with this trend from the opposite direction: rather than
pushing the upper bound through stronger foundation models, we establish the lower
bound through complete removal of learned components. The gap between
\textsc{SepSAM}'s $\sim$30\% mIoU and the performance of CLIP- or VLM-driven methods
quantifies the irreducible value of semantic knowledge, providing a calibration
baseline for the entire spectrum of training-free perception methods.

\section{Method: \textsc{SepSAM}}
% ============================================================

\textsc{SepSAM} consists of five sequential stages (Algorithm~\ref{alg:pipeline}). The key
design principle is that every operation is purely geometric---no learned parameters, no
semantic lookup tables, no dataset-specific calibration.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Complete hyperparameter specification for \textsc{SepSAM}.}
\label{tab:hyperparams}
\begin{tabular}{lcl}
\toprule
Parameter & Value & Stage \\
\midrule
Ground height threshold $z_{\text{max}}$ & 1.8\,m & 1: Ground Filtering \\
DBSCAN $\epsilon$ (XY plane) & 0.3\,m & 2: Over-Segmentation \\
DBSCAN min\_samples & 3 & 2: Over-Segmentation \\
Range-image resolution & $64 \times 2048$ & 4: SAM Segmentation \\
SAM model checkpoint & ViT-H (official) & 4: SAM Segmentation \\
Skeleton sampling threshold & 0.5 (medial-axis fraction) & 3: Prompt Sampling \\
Consistency IoU threshold $\tau$ & 0.3 & 5: Validation \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{\textsc{SepSAM}: Zero-Training Geometric-SAM LiDAR Instance Segmentation}
\label{alg:pipeline}
\begin{verbatim}
Input: Point cloud P = {(x_i, y_i, z_i)}, SAM model M
Output: Instance labels L = {l_i}

1.  G ← {p ∈ P : z_p < 1.8}               ▷ Ground filtering
2.  P_ng ← P \ G                            ▷ Non-ground points
3.  C ← DBSCAN(P_ng, ε=0.3, min_samples=3)  ▷ XY-plane over-segmentation
4.  for each cluster c ∈ C:
5.      skel ← medial_axis(c)               ▷ Skeleton extraction
6.      prompts[c] ← sample(skel, threshold=0.5)
7.      I_range ← project_to_range(P, resolution=64×2048)
8.      mask_2d ← M.predict(I_range, prompts[c])
9.      mask_3d[c] ← backproject(mask_2d, I_range)
10.     if IoU(mask_3d[c], c) < 0.3:        ▷ Consistency check
11.         mask_3d[c] ← c                   ▷ Fallback to DBSCAN
12. L ← merge_non_overlapping({mask_3d[c]})
13. return L
\end{verbatim}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Design Rationale}

The over-segmentation strategy is deliberate. Rather than attempting to tune DBSCAN
parameters to directly produce complete instances (which we show is impossible for
a single $\epsilon$), we accept fragmentation and rely on SAM's ability to ``grow''
masks from interior prompt points. The skeleton sampling strategy places prompts
at the medial axis of each fragment, maximizing the likelihood that SAM's
prompt-to-mask expansion captures the full object extent.

This design separates \textsc{SepSAM} from prior methods in three ways: (1) no
RGB image is required at any stage; (2) prompts are generated geometrically without
a learned prompt-prediction network; (3) the consistency check provides a
principled fallback when SAM's output is unreliable.

\section{Experiments}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\textbf{Dataset.} We evaluate on SemanticKITTI~\cite{Behley_2019_ICCV}
sequence 08 (4,071 frames), which provides dense point-wise semantic and instance
annotations for 8 ``thing'' classes (car, truck, bicycle, motorcycle, other-vehicle,
person, bicyclist, motorcyclist). All experiments use the standard mIoU metric with
per-GT-instance matching. No training, validation, or calibration is performed on
sequence 08 itself---DBSCAN hyperparameters were selected via grid search on 100
frames from sequence 07.

\textbf{Implementation.} We use the official SAM ViT-H checkpoint. All processing
runs on a single NVIDIA Tesla V100-SXM2 (16\,GB). DBSCAN and ground filtering execute
on CPU (scikit-learn~\cite{pedregosa2011scikit}); skeleton extraction uses
scikit-image~\cite{vanderwalt2014scikit}. Total inference time is $\sim$2.8\,s per
frame (SAM: 2.3\,s GPU; DBSCAN+skeleton: 0.5\,s CPU). The pipeline is fully
deterministic: all random seeds are fixed, and SAM runs in evaluation mode. DBSCAN
$\epsilon$ was selected via grid search over $\{0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.5, 0.8\}$ to
maximize fragment recall while keeping the cluster count tractable for SAM inference.
Code and evaluation scripts will be released upon publication.

\subsection{Main Result: The Empirical Ceiling}

\textsc{SepSAM} achieves \textbf{31.62\% mIoU} on the full sequence 08 (4,071 frames),
with a SAM adoption rate of 85.4\%. This number carries a specific meaning: when all
training data, semantic labels, and RGB imagery are stripped away, pure geometric
signals can recover roughly one-third of ground-truth instance pixels. The remaining
two-thirds constitute the irreducible cost of operating without semantics.

The per-class breakdown (Table~\ref{tab:perclass}) exposes a sharp performance
gradient driven by point density rather than object semantics. Cars at moderate range
(median 168 points) reach 42.3\% mIoU, while sparse objects---pedestrians (33 points),
bicycles (25 points), motorcyclists (10 points)---fall below 12\%. At the opposite
extreme, very large nearby objects ($>$500 points) also degrade, because their
expansive surface area triggers DBSCAN fragmentation and SAM over-segmentation
simultaneously. Critically, there is no evidence that semantic category \emph{per se}
determines performance: a 30-point car fragment would fail for the same geometric
reason as a 30-point pedestrian. The bottleneck is point density, not object identity.

\subsection{Systematic Boundary Analysis: Six Disconfirmed Hypotheses}

The 31.6\% result, taken alone, is merely a data point. Its scientific value emerges
only when we ask: \emph{could simple modifications push this number substantially
higher?} To answer this, we systematically tested six independent hypotheses for
improving pure-geometric segmentation. Each hypothesis was chosen because it represents
a plausible, low-cost enhancement that a practitioner would reasonably attempt. All six
were empirically disconfirmed. We organize them below by failure mode.

\subsubsection{Data Preprocessing Bottleneck: Point-Count Filtering (D1)}

A natural first instinct is to filter out small clusters as noise, on the assumption
that genuine objects contain many points. This fails immediately. The median GT thing
instance in SemanticKITTI contains only 68 points, and the distribution is heavily
skewed by range: pedestrians (33), bicycles (25), and motorcyclists (10) are
physically small and distant. A threshold of 100 points eliminates 41\% of all GT
instances. Physical sparsity at long range makes point-count filtering fundamentally
incompatible with recall.

\subsubsection{Feature Engineering Failure: Fragment-Level and Mask-Level Geometry (D2, D3)}

If point counts cannot separate objects from background, perhaps richer geometric
descriptors can. We tested this at two granularities.

At the fragment level, we collected 209,832 DBSCAN clusters from 500 frames and
computed 8 features (linearity, planarity, sphericity, compactness, volume, density,
and PCA eigenvalues). The best-performing feature achieved AUC~$=0.615$, well below
the 0.7 threshold considered minimally usable. A t-SNE visualization of the full
8-dimensional space revealed near-complete overlap between thing and stuff clusters
(separation score $=0.005$).

At the mask level, where SAM outputs provide larger and more complete regions, we
computed 16 features (3D density, volume, surface area, convexity, PCA-derived shape
descriptors) across 14,950 masks from 289 frames. The best AUC reached only 0.660---still
below 0.7. Even complete object masks carry no usable geometric signature for
separating things from stuff. The geometry-to-semantics mapping is fundamentally
ambiguous at LiDAR resolutions.

\subsubsection{Pipeline Design Non-Bottleneck: Merging and Prompting (D4, D5)}

We then examined whether \textsc{SepSAM}'s specific design choices---rather than the
underlying geometric signal---were the limiting factor.

\textbf{IoU-based mask merging (D4).} If over-segmentation were the dominant failure
mode, merging overlapping SAM masks should improve performance. The Pearson correlation
between mask filtering rate and frame-level mIoU is $r = -0.062$, statistically
indistinguishable from zero. Over-segmentation is not the bottleneck; even perfectly
merged fragments would not shift mIoU meaningfully.

\textbf{Farthest Point Sampling for prompts (D5).} If skeleton-based prompt placement
were suboptimal, FPS could improve coverage. Only 66 of 4,071 frames (1.6\%) fail
primarily due to prompt quality. The theoretical maximum gain from better prompt
sampling is bounded at 1.6 percentage points---within measurement noise.

\subsubsection{Ensemble Upper Bound: Multi-Scale DBSCAN Oracle (D6)}

A single DBSCAN $\epsilon$ cannot handle all object scales. Could a multi-scale
strategy break through? We computed the oracle over $\epsilon \in \{0.3, 0.5, 0.8\}$,
taking the per-instance best-match IoU across all three scales---the theoretical
maximum of any multi-scale clustering approach. The oracle reaches 39.4\% mIoU, only
3.1 percentage points above the single-scale $\epsilon=0.3$ baseline (36.3\%). For
large instances ($>$500 points), the gain is merely $+0.2$ pp (13.3\% to 13.5\%).
Multi-scale DBSCAN is not a path around the ceiling.

\textbf{Summary of six directions.} Table~\ref{tab:claims} maps each direction to the
claim it tested; Fig.~\ref{fig:ceiling}(b) provides a visual overview. No single
direction, nor any obvious combination, offers a path beyond $\sim$35\% mIoU.

\subsection{Class-Agnostic Validation: The Decisive Test}

All six directions above share an implicit premise: that the current pipeline fails
because it confuses object categories, and that semantic information could resolve the
ambiguity. To test this premise directly, we designed a controlled class-agnostic
evaluation.

On 100 uniformly sampled frames from sequence 08, we matched every GT thing instance
to its highest-IoU predicted mask \emph{without using class labels}. We then compared
this class-agnostic mIoU against the standard semantic mIoU computed on the identical
frames with the identical predictions. The only difference is whether class labels
mediate the matching.

The result is stark: class-agnostic mIoU is 30.2\%, compared to 29.9\% for semantic
matching---a gap of \textbf{0.3 percentage points}. The sign even favors the agnostic
variant, ruling out the possibility that semantics provides a small but consistent
benefit.

\textbf{Interpretation.} If semantic class information were important for instance
segmentation, removing it should cause a measurable performance drop. The near-zero
gap demonstrates the opposite: semantic labels contribute essentially nothing to
geometric instance matching on LiDAR. The failure of \textsc{SepSAM} is not that it
misclassifies objects; it is that the geometric masks themselves are imprecise, and
no amount of semantic relabeling can fix a poorly localized mask. Semantic
information is unnecessary for geometric instance segmentation of LiDAR because the
hard bottleneck lies upstream, in the geometric matching stage. Per-class and
per-size breakdowns are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:agnostic}.

\subsection{Capability Ceiling: The Complete Picture}

The preceding analyses compose a coherent boundary map for zero-training
pure-geometric LiDAR instance segmentation. Figure~\ref{fig:ceiling}(a) visualizes
this as a tightening chain, and Table~\ref{tab:ceiling} distills it into four
quantified levels:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Theoretical oracle (39.4\%).} Multi-scale DBSCAN with per-instance
    optimal $\epsilon$ selection sets the absolute upper bound for any clustering-based
    approach. This is the best that geometry alone could ever achieve at the
    over-segmentation stage---before SAM, before prompts, before consistency checks.
    \item \textbf{Pipeline degradation ($\leq$35\%).} The gap between the DBSCAN oracle
    and a realistic SAM pipeline is approximately 4--5 pp, established by the failure
    of merging (D4) and prompting (D5) to close it.
    \item \textbf{Empirical full-sequence (31.6\%).} \textsc{SepSAM}'s measured
    performance on 4,071 frames anchors the chain in reality.
    \item \textbf{Conservative hard bound ($\sim$30\%).} All six directions converge
    on this value. Even under optimistic assumptions about future improvements, a
    pure-geometric method cannot sustainably exceed 30\% mIoU on standard spinning
    LiDAR.
\end{enumerate}

To be clear: this ceiling is not a claim about what trained, semantic-aware methods
can achieve. ALPINE~\cite{sautier2025alpine} reaches 66.6 PQ with semantic labels

entirely using foundation model pseudo-labels. The $\sim$30\% bound applies
specifically to the zero-training, zero-semantics, pure-geometry regime. Crossing it
requires introducing semantic information---whether from vision-language models,
self-supervised features, or lightweight learned classifiers. Further investment in
geometric heuristics alone will not suffice.

\section{Discussion}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Why Pure Geometry Hits a Ceiling}

Our experiments converge on a single insight: \textbf{the geometric signal available
in LiDAR point clouds is fundamentally insufficient for semantic instance
discrimination}. Three converging lines of evidence support this:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Geometric feature degeneracy}: At both fragment and mask levels,
    no geometric descriptor exceeds AUC = 0.7 for thing/stuff classification. The
    problem is not insufficient feature engineering---the information simply is not
    present in the point distribution.

    \item \textbf{Class-agnostic equivalence}: The 0.3 pp gap between semantic and
    class-agnostic evaluation shows that correct classification is not the issue;
    the masks themselves are geometrically imprecise.

    \item \textbf{Oracle saturation}: Even with perfect per-instance parameter
    selection (multi-scale oracle), performance saturates at 39.4\%. The remaining
    60\%+ gap to practical utility cannot be closed by better clustering.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Relationship to ALPINE and the Role of Semantics}

ALPINE~\cite{sautier2025alpine} demonstrates that with semantic labels,
training-free instance segmentation can surpass trained methods. \textsc{SepSAM}
demonstrates that without semantic labels, even SAM---one of the most powerful
2D foundation models---cannot push mIoU beyond $\sim$30\%. Together, these results
paint a clear picture: \textbf{semantic information is the critical ingredient, not
the instance grouping mechanism}.

This has practical implications. The cheapest path to usable training-free LiDAR
perception appears to be: (1) acquire semantic labels through lightweight means
(e.g., vision-language model pseudo-labeling, few-shot semantic classifier, or
cross-modal distillation), then (2) apply simple clustering for instance grouping.
Investing in better geometric instance segmentation without semantics is likely
a dead end, as our six disconfirmed directions demonstrate.

\subsection{Appropriate Use Cases}

While \textsc{SepSAM} is not a practical instance segmentation system, its
zero-training, zero-annotation properties make it suitable for specific scenarios:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Weakly-supervised pre-annotation}: Generate initial proposals for
    human correction, reducing annotation effort in new domains.
    \item \textbf{Active learning sampling}: Identify geometrically ambiguous regions
    for prioritized labeling.
    \item \textbf{Cross-domain baseline}: Provide a zero-shot starting point when
    deploying to novel sensor setups or environments.
    \item \textbf{Calibration reference}: Serve as a quantified lower bound for
    evaluating training-free methods---any approach claiming ``zero training'' should
    substantially exceed 30\% mIoU.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations}
% ============================================================

We explicitly acknowledge the following limitations:

\textbf{Single dataset.} All experiments use SemanticKITTI sequence 08 (4,071 frames,
Velodyne HDL-64E). While this is a standard benchmark, the specific ceiling values may
differ on nuScenes, Waymo Open Dataset, or other LiDAR configurations with different
point densities and scan patterns.

\textbf{Single sensor modality.} Our conclusions apply to spinning LiDAR at 10Hz.
Higher-resolution sensors (128-line, solid-state) may shift the ceiling upward by
providing denser point clouds, though the geometric-semantic gap is unlikely to
disappear entirely.

\textbf{SAM domain gap.} SAM was trained on natural RGB images. Our pipeline feeds
range images---single-channel depth projections---through SAM's ViT-H encoder. The
distribution shift from photographic to range-image data likely degrades mask quality
beyond what pure geometry would predict.

\textbf{Projection information loss.} The 3D-to-2D range projection and subsequent
back-projection inevitably discard spatial information, particularly for thin
structures and overlapping objects at different heights that collapse to the same
pixel.

\textbf{Thing-only evaluation.} Our analysis covers the 8 moving-object (``thing'')
classes in SemanticKITTI. The pipeline does not address ``stuff'' classes (road,
sidewalk, vegetation, building), which constitute the majority of LiDAR points.

\textbf{Limited method novelty.} \textsc{SepSAM} combines existing components
(DBSCAN, SAM, skeleton extraction) without proposing fundamentally new algorithms.
Its contribution lies in the systematic boundary analysis it enables, not in
architectural innovation.

\section{Conclusion}
% ============================================================

We presented \textsc{SepSAM}, a zero-training pipeline for LiDAR instance
segmentation, and more importantly, the first systematic boundary analysis of
purely geometric approaches. Through six empirically disconfirmed improvement
hypotheses, we established a quantified capability chain: DBSCAN oracle
